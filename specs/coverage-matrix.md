**Flag — grammar the books consistently teach but our plan omits or under-specifies:**

1. **be going to** — taught by **4/4 books** as the standard A0-A1 future. We substitute Present Continuous for future (L7). Defensible, but a "complete" claim is weaker without `going to`, since it's the more frequent everyday future ("I'm going to call the doctor"). **Recommend adding it as a second form inside L7 or L15.**
2. **Object pronouns** — **4/4 books** teach them; our map nominally includes them (topic #4) but **no built or planned lesson actually delivers them**. This is a real hole, not a theme cut. Needs a confirmed home (logically L6/L7 once verbs exist: "I pick **them**", "give **me** the crate").
3. **like/love/hate + -ing** — 3/4 teach the -ing complement explicitly; our L15 risks teaching only "I like tea" and missing "I like **picking**".

Everything else: our 15-lesson plan maps the CEFR grammar **1:1 and completely**. Grammar scope is our strongest area.

**Verdict on this section:** the four books make functional language a **first-class, per-unit component**. We have **no functional layer at all** — functions are buried in dialogue lines and never isolated as reusable scripts ("Excuse me, where is…?", "Can I have…?", "Sorry, I don't understand"). For an audience whose entire goal is *survival transactions*, this is our **single biggest gap against a recommendable A0-A1 resource** — more damaging than any grammar omission.

**Concrete must-adds:** the **alphabet/spelling-your-name** function, a **café/shop ordering script**, a **time-asking script**, and a **"I don't understand / please repeat / how do you spell that"** classroom-survival block (taught by all four as classroom language — we have *zero*).

## 4. Skills balance

| Skill | What the books do | What we do | Gap |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Listening** | Audio per unit; sub-skills (word stress, contractions, can/can't, linking) — f2f & Speakout have explicit "Help with Listening" / pronunciation strands | **Web Speech TTS** on every word, example, dialogue line (`en-GB`, rate 0.88). Input only. | We have audio **output** but **no listening *task*** (no "listen and choose / gap-fill"). Pronunciation/minimal-pairs drilling absent. |
| **Reading** | Graded reading texts per unit + gist/detail comprehension | Learners **decode** Latin + IPA + Cyrillic on screen (a core design goal) | **No reading-for-gist/detail task.** Decoding ≠ comprehension. The CEFR reading sub-skill is unpractised. |
| **Speaking** | Role-plays, pair work, info-gap in every unit | **Not supported** — no speech recognition; learners hear models only | Hardest to close (privacy/tech). Books make this central; we can't assess output. |
| **Writing** | Capital letters, linkers, emails, forms, blog/forum entries; portfolios | **Not supported** — no text input anywhere; only tap-to-select quiz | No productive output of any kind. |

**What a credible A0-A1 platform needs beyond grammar + MCQ quiz:**
- **At least one true listening task** (listen → pick the right reply) — trivially buildable on our existing TTS.
- **At least one reading-for-gist micro-task** — show the dialogue, ask one RU comprehension question.
- A **controlled "production" surrogate** for speaking/writing: *tap-to-build a sentence from word tiles* (drag/select order). This gives quasi-productive practice without a keyboard or microphone — fits low digital literacy and offline.

Right now we exercise **recognition only** (MCQ). All four reference books balance recognition with at least *guided* production. That imbalance is the second-biggest credibility gap after the functional layer.

## 6. Verdict

**Is the current 15-lesson plan sufficient for "complete A0-A1"? — On grammar, essentially yes. As a recommendable *resource*, not yet.**

- **Grammar:** our plan covers the CEFR A0-A1 outline **1:1**. The only genuine grammar holes are **object pronouns (no real home)** and **be going to (cut)** — both fixable inside existing lessons. This pillar is solid.
- **Vocabulary:** strong and well farm-adapted; the only material gaps are **adjectives** and **nationality adjectives** — both trivial to add. Sports cut is justified.
- **Functional / Everyday English:** **this is the disqualifying gap.** All four reference books make functional survival language a per-unit, first-class component. We currently teach **no function as a reusable script**, and we have **no alphabet/spelling and no "I don't understand" classroom-survival language at all** — the highest-utility items for our exact audience.
- **Skills:** we are **recognition-only** (TTS output + MCQ). No listening *task*, no reading comprehension, no production. Every reference book balances recognition with guided production.

**Minimal set that makes it genuinely recommendable** (the floor, not the wish-list):

1. **Give object pronouns a confirmed lesson** (a, #1) and **add be going to** (a, #6) — closes the grammar.
2. **Add an "Everyday English" functional block to every lesson** (b, #9) + the **"I don't understand / spell-your-name / alphabet"** survival language (b, #11, a, #2) — closes the single biggest gap and is the product's whole reason for existing.
3. **Ship the survival phrasebook** (b, #10) — already promised in `structure.md`, currently vapour.
4. **Add one listening-choose task and one reading-gist question per lesson** (b, #12-13) — both near-free on existing content; turns "grammar+quiz" into a balanced course.
5. **Add a review/SRS mode** (b, #15) — without retention, a one-lesson-a-day A0 learner never reaches A1.

Items 1-4 are mostly LOW/MED effort and reuse existing content. With them, the project moves from "a well-structured grammar syllabus with quizzes" to "a complete, recommendable A0-A1 survival course." The **tap-to-build production task (#14)** and **certificate (#16)** are the next tier — high value for credibility and the TikTok funnel, but not strictly required for an A0-A1 completeness claim.
